Impact of Air Quality Policies on Public Health in Southeast Asia

After the major haze crises in 2013 in Singapore and Malaysia, public awareness of PM2.5 pollution significantly increased. The health impacts of fine particulate matter became more apparent, leading to heightened concerns across Southeast Asia. This prompted regional governments and organizations like ASEAN to take action. In this project, we aim to highlight the differences in health outcomes before and after the implementation of air quality policies, showcasing the effectiveness of these measures in reducing the health burden caused by PM2.5, and emphasizing the importance of continued efforts to protect public health in the region.

Key Milestones in Southeast Asia's Efforts to Combat PM2.5 Pollution

2010-2015

Rising public awareness, particularly after major haze crises in 2013 in Singapore and Malaysia, which drew attention to fine particulate matter. The WHO began highlighting the health risks of PM2.5.

2015

ASEAN strengthened efforts to combat transboundary haze pollution, especially from Indonesia, with a focus on PM2.5. Countries like Thailand and Indonesia began implementing national standards for PM2.5.

2016-2020

Improved monitoring and policy enforcement across the region, with countries like Thailand and Indonesia introducing air quality standards and expanding public access to air quality data.
